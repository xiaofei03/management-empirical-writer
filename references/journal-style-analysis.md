# Journal Style Analysis

Use this reference when the user provides journal samples or target publication goals.

## Goal

Profile how the Chinese and English drafts should differ in framing, structure, and rhetoric while preserving the same evidence base.

## Inputs

- Target journal name or journal family
- Sample papers, ideally 2 to 5
- User preferences from advisor, thesis office, or journal guide

## What to Analyze

### Structure

- Typical section order
- Whether literature review and hypotheses are merged or separated
- Whether discussion is independent or folded into conclusion
- Typical abstract structure

### Contribution framing

- Does the journal emphasize problem significance, theory contribution, policy value, managerial value, or methodological novelty?
- How explicit is the gap statement?
- How early is the contribution stated?

### Evidence presentation

- How much space is given to research design?
- How detailed are robustness and endogeneity discussions?
- How are mechanisms framed?
- How concise or expansive are result interpretations?

### Tone and language

- Formality level
- Directness of claims
- Use of signaling language such as "contribute," "suggest," "indicate," or "we find"
- Use of policy or managerial framing

## Practical Guidance

- If the Chinese target is unclear, default to a solid domestic management or economics journal style rather than a thesis-only style.
- If the English target is unclear, default to a cautious SSCI management-journal style that foregrounds gap, contribution, and identification.
- Never mimic a sample paper's claims. Only adapt its structure, pacing, and rhetoric.

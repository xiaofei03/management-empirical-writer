# Introduction and Theory Development Writing Rules

Use this reference when drafting, revising, or reviewing the first two chapters of an empirical management paper:

- `Introduction`
- `引言`
- `Theoretical Background`
- `理论基础`
- `Theory Development`
- `研究假设`
- literature-review sections that directly support hypotheses

The purpose is to make the first two chapters do different jobs:

- Introduction answers: why does this research problem matter?
- Theory Development answers: why should the proposed theoretical relationships hold?

Do not let the two chapters repeat the same background, literature list, or theory definitions.

## Core Principle

The first two chapters should build a problem-to-theory bridge, not a literature pile.

Default division of labor:

- Introduction: phenomenon, tension, gap, theoretical lens, data preview, findings preview, contribution.
- Theory Development: theory source, core mechanism, theory-to-variable mapping, hypothesis logic.

If the introduction already explains the full mechanism in detail, reduce it. If the theory chapter only repeats the introduction, rebuild it around theory mapping and hypothesis logic.

## Introduction Rules

### 1. Opening Paragraph: Core Phenomenon

Goal: establish the research problem quickly.

Recommended structure:

```text
real-world phenomenon -> tension or contradiction -> research object/problem
```

The first paragraph should answer only: what important problem is happening?

Avoid:

- beginning with broad macro-era slogans
- long lists of policy, industry, or technology terms
- early theory definitions
- previewing all mechanisms, moderators, and contributions

### 2. Existing Literature and Gap

Do not write a serial list such as "A studied..., B studied..., C studied...".

Summarize existing research by theme, such as:

- concept definition
- antecedents
- main consequences
- mechanisms
- boundary conditions

Then state the gap in direct relation to the research question:

- what relationship is still unclear?
- why is the mechanism still underexplained?
- under what conditions might the relationship change?

The gap must not be a generic "few studies have examined..." claim unless it is tied to a concrete theoretical or empirical blind spot.

### 3. Explain Why the Outcome Matters

Do not merely say the dependent variable is important.

Use this bridge:

```text
outcome importance
-> organizational foundations of that outcome
-> how the focal explanatory variable may influence those foundations
-> why the relationship deserves study
```

For example, if the outcome is resilience, explain what resilience depends on, then show how the focal phenomenon may affect those foundations.

### 4. Introduce the Theoretical Lens After the Problem

Theory should appear after the phenomenon and gap are clear.

The theory paragraph must answer:

- why this theory is especially suitable for the focal problem
- what aspect of the phenomenon the theory explains better than plausible alternatives
- how the theory connects the independent variable, mechanism, moderator, and outcome

Do not introduce theory only by reciting its definition.

### 5. Data, Findings, and Contributions

The introduction should briefly state:

- data source
- sample scope
- core variable construction
- main findings

Contribution writing should be specific:

- theoretical contribution: which theoretical conversation is advanced, and how?
- literature contribution: which research stream receives a new explanation or boundary?
- mechanism contribution: which pathway or process is newly identified?

Avoid generic expressions such as "enriches the literature" or "has important significance" unless the sentence specifies exactly what is enriched.

## Theory Development Rules

### General Logic

Theory Development should not re-introduce the whole background. It should explain why each hypothesis is theoretically plausible.

Default logic:

```text
theoretical concept
-> variable role
-> mechanism
-> hypothesis
```

### Theory Introduction Structure

When introducing a theory, cover four steps:

1. Source: who proposed the theory, and what problem did it originally solve?
2. Core mechanism: what are the key elements and causal logic?
3. Management extension: how has the theory been used to explain organizational phenomena?
4. Article fit: how does the theory map onto this paper's variables and setting?

For signaling-style theories, explicitly identify:

- sender
- signal
- receiver
- signal cost
- signal credibility
- feedback or verification environment
- unobservable quality being inferred

### Theory-to-Variable Mapping

Before writing hypotheses, make the mapping explicit:

- Who is the theoretical actor?
- What is the key behavior or signal?
- Who receives, interprets, or responds to it?
- What quality or state is difficult to observe directly?
- Which mediator represents which theoretical process?
- Which moderator changes which part of the process?

If this mapping cannot be answered, the theory section is not ready.

## Hypothesis Derivation Template

Each hypothesis should contain:

```text
theoretical basis
-> operating mechanism
-> expected direction
-> boundary or competing force when relevant
-> hypothesis statement
```

Do not start from the expected empirical result and then search backward for theory.

## Nonlinear Relationship Rules

A nonlinear hypothesis cannot stop at "low levels are beneficial, high levels are harmful."

It must explain why the direction changes.

Recommended structure:

```text
early stage: benefits exceed costs
turning stage: benefits decline or costs accelerate
late stage: costs exceed benefits
```

Possible benefit channels:

- resource access
- relationship building
- attention or cognition
- legitimacy or endorsement
- learning or capability mobilization

Possible cost channels:

- credibility loss
- relationship damage
- governance pressure
- resource misallocation
- monitoring or sanction costs

The key question is: what makes the marginal effect change sign?

## Mediation Writing Rules

Do not write mediation as:

```text
X affects M, and M affects Y.
```

That is a statistical chain, not a theoretical mechanism.

Write mediation as:

```text
X changes a theoretical process
-> the mediator captures that process
-> the process changes Y
```

The mediator must answer: which theoretical link does it represent?

## Moderation Writing Rules

Do not infer moderation only from result direction.

A moderator must change a specific part of the theoretical process, such as:

- information acquisition
- information interpretation
- signal verification
- resource response
- behavioral constraint
- environmental contingency

Correct logic:

```text
Z changes how X operates through a theory-defined process.
```

Weak logic:

```text
Z makes the result stronger or weaker.
```

## Citation Use Rules

Citations should serve the argument, not display reading volume.

Each cited paper should play at least one role:

- define a concept
- support the theory
- justify a mechanism
- establish a gap
- validate measurement
- motivate a boundary condition

Avoid one-shot citation piles. A normal citation group should usually contain 1-3 references; 4-5 references require a clear shared argumentative function; more than 5 should be split by claim function or moved to a literature-map discussion.

Foundational older references are appropriate when they are original theory, classic constructs, methods, or measurement sources. Do not replace a foundational source with a newer secondary citation merely to make the reference list look recent.

## First-Two-Chapter Review Checklist

After reading the first two chapters, a reviewer should be able to answer:

- Why is the research problem important?
- What exactly is missing in prior research?
- Why is the chosen theory appropriate?
- How are the theory's actors, signals, mechanisms, and boundary conditions mapped to the paper's variables?
- Why should each hypothesis hold?
- If a relationship is nonlinear, why does the effect change direction?
- If a mediator is used, what theoretical process does it capture?
- If a moderator is used, which theoretical link does it alter?
- Are citations used for defined argumentative roles rather than padding?
- Do Introduction and Theory Development have distinct functions without repetitive background?

If these questions cannot be answered, the first two chapters are still descriptive rather than theory-developing.
